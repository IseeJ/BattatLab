Neutrino Detection and Particle Astrophysics

    Our Universe is fascinating and mysterious. Through different lenses, we each try to make sense of it all. In our lab, we focus on particle astrophysics, and use (and develop new) experimental techniques to make precision measurements of neutrino properties. Our current focus is on the development of lab-scale liquid argon time projection chamber to support the Q-Pix consortium's development of pixelated readout technology for neutrino detection.

    Neutrinos are the most abundant elementary particles with mass in the Universe (trillions of them passed through us per second!). They come from many sources for example, from the nuclear fusion of the Sun, during supernova explosions, in the Earth’s atmosphere when cosmic rays enters, and in nuclear reactors. Some neutrinos may even come from the Big Bang, making them some of the oldest messengers in the Universe!

    In the Standard Model of particle physics (our best guide to understanding tiny particles), neutrinos belong to a family called leptons. That means they don’t take part in the strong force, which is the force that holds atomic nuclei together. Neutrinos are electrically neutral, extremely light, and don’t interact much with other matter — kind of like cosmic ghosts. That’s why they’re so hard to detect, and we are still trying to uncover their secrets!

Purity Monitor

    The purity monitor consists of a field cage that generates a uniform electric field. Electrons are generated at the cathode of the field cage via the photoelectric effect. A series of short ultra-violet (UV) light pulses from a Xenon flash lamp is transmitted through a fiber optic cable to the photocathode positioned at the center of the field cage, stripping electrons from it. The photoelectrons produced then drift upward through LAr from the cathode and are collected at the anode. Wire grids are placed on both ends of the field cage to shield the cathode and anode from induced current. Charge-sensitive amplifiers are connected to each electrode to integrate the current induced by drifting electrons once arrive, allowing us to quantify the amount of charge produced at the cathode and arrived at the anode.
