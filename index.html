Neutrino Detection and Particle Astrophysics

    Our Universe is fascinating and mysterious. Through different lenses, we each try to make sense of it all. In our lab, we focus on particle astrophysics, and use (and develop new) experimental techniques to make precision measurements of neutrino properties. Our current focus is on the development of lab-scale liquid argon time projection chamber to support the Q-Pix consortium's development of pixelated readout technology for neutrino detection.

Neutrino

    Neutrinos are the most abundant elementary particles with mass in the Universe (trillions of them passed through us per second!). They come from many sources for example, from the nuclear fusion of the Sun, during supernova explosions, in the Earth’s atmosphere when cosmic rays enters, and in nuclear reactors. Some neutrinos may even come from the Big Bang, making them some of the oldest messengers in the Universe! In the Standard Model of particle physics (our best guide to understanding tiny particles), neutrinos belong to a family called leptons. That means they don’t take part in the strong force, which is the force that holds atomic nuclei together. Neutrinos are electrically neutral, extremely light, and don’t interact much with other matter — kind of like cosmic ghosts. That’s why they’re so hard to detect, and we are still trying to uncover their secrets!
